## Reddit Post (r/emergencymedicine)

**Title:** The 5 documentation mistakes I see ER docs make that plaintiff attorneys love

**Body:**

I have been an emergency physician for 20+ years, and I have reviewed hundreds of charts as an expert witness. Here are the five documentation patterns I see repeatedly that make plaintiff attorneys salivate:

**1. The Missing Pertinent Negative**

You rule out PE but never document that the patient denied calf pain, prior DVT, or recent immobilization. Your chart says you thought about it. The jury sees no evidence you considered it.

Example of what is missing: "Patient denies unilateral leg swelling, calf tenderness, recent immobilization, prior DVT/PE, OCP use, or recent surgery."

**2. The "Improved, Discharge" Without Reasoning**

You bring the troponin back negative, the patient feels better, and you write "improved, discharge." But you never documented WHY the patient is safe to go home. A 52-year-old with chest pain and three risk factors needs more than "improved."

What a plaintiff attorney reads: "The doctor did not document why this patient was safe to send home."

What you should add: "Patient meets discharge criteria based on serial negative troponins at 0 and 3 hours, HEART score of 3 (low risk), symptom resolution with GI cocktail, and normal ECG. Discussed with patient that cardiac workup was reassuring but symptoms should be followed up with PCP within 48 hours."

**3. Time Gaps in Critical Presentations**

Your first note is at 14:02. The CT result comes back at 14:47. Your disposition note is at 16:30. What happened in that 103-minute gap? If a complication occurs during that window, you have no contemporaneous documentation.

Fix: Brief reassessment notes — even one line — at key decision points.

**4. Vague Return Precautions**

"Return if worse" is the medicolegal equivalent of "good luck." Your return precautions should be specific enough that a reasonable person knows what to watch for and when to come back.

Bad: "Return to ED if symptoms worsen."
Better: "Return to ED immediately if: chest pain returns or worsens, new shortness of breath, lightheadedness, diaphoresis, pain radiating to jaw or arm. Call 911 for severe symptoms — do not drive yourself."

**5. The Undocumented Shared Decision-Making**

You talked to the patient for 15 minutes about the risks and benefits of discharge. They understood. They agreed. But none of it is in the chart. When the case goes to litigation 3 years later, there is no evidence that conversation happened.

Add: "Discussed with patient the differential diagnosis including [X, Y, Z], the significance of their test results, and the plan for follow-up. Patient verbalizes understanding of return precautions and agrees with discharge plan."
