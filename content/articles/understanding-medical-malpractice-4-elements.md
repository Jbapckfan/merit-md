## What Is Medical Malpractice?

If you have been harmed by medical care and are considering a lawsuit, one of the most important things to understand is what your attorney must prove in court. Medical malpractice is not simply a bad outcome or an unpleasant experience. It is a specific legal claim with four required elements, and your attorney must prove **all four** to win your case.

These four elements are: **duty, breach, causation, and damages**. Think of them as four links in a chain -- if any one link is missing, the chain breaks and the case cannot succeed.

This article explains each element in plain language so you know what to expect, what your attorney will be working on, and why certain cases are stronger than others.

## Element 1: Duty -- Did the Doctor Owe You a Duty of Care?

The first thing your attorney must establish is that a **physician-patient relationship** existed. This relationship is what creates the doctor's legal duty to provide you with competent medical care.

In most cases, this element is straightforward. If you went to a hospital or clinic, were seen by a doctor, and received treatment (or should have received treatment), a physician-patient relationship existed. The duty was established.

**When duty might be in question:**

- You called a nurse hotline and were given advice but never went to the hospital
- A doctor gave informal advice to a friend or family member outside a clinical setting
- You were in the waiting room but left before being seen by a physician
- An on-call specialist was consulted by phone but claims they never assumed care

In emergency room cases, duty is almost always clear. Under a federal law called EMTALA (the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act), hospitals that accept Medicare are required to provide a medical screening examination to anyone who comes to the emergency department, regardless of their ability to pay. This creates a clear duty of care.

**Hospital vs. physician liability:** Even if the doctor was an independent contractor rather than a hospital employee, the hospital may still be held liable under a legal theory called "apparent agency" (sometimes called "ostensible agency"). If you reasonably believed the doctor was a hospital employee, the hospital may share responsibility for the doctor's actions.

## Element 2: Breach -- Did the Doctor Fall Below the Standard of Care?

This is often the most contested element in a malpractice case. Your attorney must show that the doctor **deviated from the accepted standard of care** -- meaning the doctor did something (or failed to do something) that a reasonably competent doctor in the same specialty would not have done under similar circumstances.

**What is the "standard of care"?**

The standard of care is not perfection. It is the level of care, skill, and treatment that a reasonably competent physician in the same specialty would provide in a similar situation. It is based on:

- Published medical guidelines from organizations like the American Heart Association or the American College of Emergency Physicians
- Hospital protocols and policies
- Generally accepted medical practice within the specialty
- The resources and information available to the doctor at the time

**Examples of breach:**

- An ER doctor sends a patient home with chest pain after drawing only one troponin (a heart attack blood test), when the standard of care requires serial troponin testing
- A surgeon operates on the wrong side of the body
- A doctor fails to order a CT scan for a patient with signs of a stroke
- A nurse gives the wrong medication or the wrong dose
- A doctor fails to read or act on abnormal test results
- A hospital discharges a patient with sepsis (a serious blood infection) without administering antibiotics

**How breach is proven:** Your attorney will hire a medical expert -- a physician in the same specialty as the doctor who treated you -- to review your records and provide an opinion on whether the standard of care was met. This expert opinion is required in virtually every state.

## Element 3: Causation -- Did the Breach Cause Your Injury?

Proving that the doctor made a mistake is not enough by itself. Your attorney must also prove that the mistake **directly caused** your injury or made it worse. This is called causation, and it has two parts:

**Cause-in-fact ("but-for" causation):** But for the doctor's action or inaction, would you have been harmed? In other words, if the doctor had done the right thing, would your outcome have been different?

For example: If an ER doctor missed your heart attack, your attorney must show that if the heart attack had been caught on time, treatment (like opening the blocked artery with a stent) would have prevented or reduced the damage to your heart.

**Proximate cause (foreseeability):** Was the injury a foreseeable consequence of the doctor's mistake? The harm must be a natural and expected result of the type of error that occurred.

**Why causation can be challenging:**

- **Pre-existing conditions.** If you already had significant heart disease, the defense may argue that your outcome would have been poor even with perfect care.
- **Multiple treating physicians.** If several doctors were involved in your care, it may be difficult to determine which doctor's actions caused the harm.
- **Delayed diagnosis cases.** When a diagnosis is missed, your attorney must show that earlier detection would have led to a better outcome -- which requires statistical evidence about survival and recovery rates.
- **The "loss of chance" doctrine.** In some states, even if the doctor's mistake did not definitely cause your injury, you may have a claim if it reduced your chances of a better outcome. For example, if you had a 40% chance of surviving a cancer and the delayed diagnosis reduced that to 15%, some states allow you to recover damages for the lost 25% chance. Not all states recognize this doctrine, and the rules vary significantly.

## Element 4: Damages -- What Harm Did You Suffer?

The final element requires showing that you suffered actual, measurable harm as a result of the doctor's mistake. Without damages, there is no case -- even if the doctor clearly made an error.

**Types of damages in medical malpractice cases:**

**Economic damages (out-of-pocket losses):**
- Past and future medical bills related to the injury
- Lost wages and lost earning capacity
- Cost of ongoing care, rehabilitation, or assistive devices
- Home modification costs (if the injury resulted in disability)

**Non-economic damages (quality-of-life losses):**
- Pain and suffering
- Emotional distress and mental anguish
- Loss of enjoyment of life
- Loss of consortium (impact on your relationship with your spouse)
- Disfigurement or disability

**Punitive damages (in rare cases):**
- Awarded only when the doctor's conduct was reckless or grossly negligent
- Not available in all states, and subject to caps where permitted

**Important note on damage caps:** Many states have laws that limit (or "cap") the amount of non-economic damages you can receive in a medical malpractice case. These caps vary widely -- some states cap non-economic damages at $250,000, while others have no cap at all. Your attorney will know the rules in your state.

## Why All Four Elements Must Be Proven

Understanding why all four elements are required helps you assess the strength of your potential case:

- **Strong duty + strong breach + weak causation = weak case.** Even if the doctor clearly made a mistake, if the outcome likely would have been the same regardless, the case may not succeed.
- **Strong breach + strong causation + minimal damages = small case.** If the doctor's error was clear and caused harm, but the harm was minor and temporary, the case may not justify the cost of litigation.
- **Clear deviation + severe harm + strong causation = strong case.** When a clear, guidelines-based protocol violation leads to a dramatically worse outcome, the case is at its strongest.

## How Your Attorney Evaluates Your Case

When you first consult a medical malpractice attorney, they will typically:

1. **Review your medical records** to understand what happened during your care
2. **Assess duty** -- usually the easiest element to establish
3. **Have an expert physician review the records** to determine whether there was a breach of the standard of care
4. **Evaluate causation** -- could better care have prevented or reduced your injury?
5. **Calculate damages** -- what are the total economic and non-economic harms you have suffered?

Most malpractice attorneys work on contingency, meaning they take a percentage of the recovery (typically 33-40%) and charge nothing if the case does not succeed. They also typically cover the upfront costs of expert review and litigation. This means you can have your case evaluated without financial risk.

## What Makes a Medical Malpractice Case Particularly Strong?

While every case is different, certain factors make a malpractice claim stronger:

- **Clear protocol violation.** When the doctor violated a specific, written guideline or hospital protocol, the breach is easier to prove.
- **Objective evidence.** Lab results, imaging studies, vital signs, and timestamps create a factual record that is difficult to dispute.
- **Severe, lasting harm.** Cases involving death, permanent disability, brain injury, or loss of limb carry the most significant damages.
- **Good documentation.** The more detailed your medical records, the easier it is to reconstruct what happened and identify where care went wrong.
- **Timely filing.** Medical malpractice claims have a statute of limitations (a deadline for filing), which varies by state. Filing promptly preserves evidence and your legal rights.

## Next Steps if You Believe You Have a Case

If you believe you or a loved one received care that fell below the standard, and that substandard care caused real harm, consider taking these steps:

1. **Request your complete medical records** from every provider involved in your care
2. **Write down your recollection** of events while they are fresh in your memory
3. **Do not discuss the case on social media** or with the provider's office
4. **Consult a medical malpractice attorney** for a professional evaluation of your case

Remember: not every bad outcome is malpractice, but every patient has the right to receive care that meets the accepted standard. If that standard was not met and you were harmed as a result, the law provides a path to accountability and compensation.
