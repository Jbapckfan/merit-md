## Overview: Acute Coronary Syndrome in the ED

Acute coronary syndrome (ACS) encompasses a spectrum of conditions -- from unstable angina to non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI) and ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) -- that result from acute myocardial ischemia. Missed ACS remains the single most common diagnostic failure leading to emergency medicine malpractice claims, accounting for approximately 20-30% of all ED malpractice payouts nationally.

The clinical stakes are high: patients discharged from the ED with unrecognized ACS face a 25% risk of adverse cardiac events within 72 hours, including death. For plaintiff attorneys, the combination of clear guidelines, objective testing protocols, and measurable deviations makes these cases among the most winnable in medical malpractice litigation.

## Current Standard of Care: Guideline Framework

The standard of care for ED chest pain evaluation is defined by the convergence of several authoritative guideline sources. While no single guideline constitutes the legal standard of care (courts consider guidelines as evidence of the standard, not the standard itself), the following are the primary references:

**AHA/ACC Guidelines (2021 Chest Pain Evaluation, updated 2024)**
- Risk stratification required for all patients with acute chest pain
- History, physical examination, and 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes of presentation
- Serial troponin testing using a validated protocol (0/1-hour, 0/3-hour, or 0/6-hour depending on assay)
- Structured risk assessment using validated tools (HEART score, TIMI, GRACE)
- Stress testing or coronary CTA for intermediate-risk patients before discharge

**ACEP Clinical Policy on Acute Coronary Syndromes (2018, reaffirmed 2024)**
- Level A recommendation: obtain a 12-lead ECG as soon as possible
- Level B recommendation: use a validated risk stratification tool
- Level B recommendation: serial troponin testing using institution-specific protocols with defined time intervals

**Surviving Sepsis Campaign / CMS Guidelines**
- Not directly applicable but relevant when ACS presents alongside sepsis or other systemic illness, requiring parallel workups

## Serial Troponin: The Linchpin of ACS Evaluation

The troponin assay is the cornerstone of ACS diagnosis. Understanding the testing protocol is essential for any plaintiff case involving missed MI.

**High-Sensitivity Troponin (hs-cTnI or hs-cTnT)**

Most hospitals in the United States have transitioned to high-sensitivity troponin assays. The standard protocols include:

- **0/1-hour protocol (ESC algorithm)**: Draw at presentation and at 1 hour. Rule-out if both values are below assay-specific thresholds and delta is below threshold. Requires a third draw at 3 hours for the "observe zone."
- **0/3-hour protocol**: Draw at presentation and at 3 hours. Rule-out if both values are below the 99th percentile URL and no significant rise/fall pattern.
- **0/6-hour protocol (conventional troponin)**: For hospitals still using conventional assays, serial draws at 0 and 6 hours minimum.

**Key Litigation Points**

- A single troponin value, regardless of sensitivity, cannot rule out ACS. This is a Level A guideline recommendation and a common deviation target.
- The "delta" (change between serial values) is as important as the absolute values. A rising trend of >20% between serial draws is considered significant, even if both values remain below the 99th percentile.
- Hospital-specific protocols and order sets serve as powerful evidence. If the institution's own protocol requires serial troponins and the physician failed to follow it, the deviation is self-evident.

## ECG Interpretation Failures

The 12-lead ECG is the first-line diagnostic tool and must be obtained within 10 minutes of presentation. Common deviations include:

**Failure to Obtain Timely ECG**
- Document the time of triage, time of ECG order, and time ECG was performed. Gaps exceeding 10 minutes from presentation require justification.

**Failure to Recognize STEMI Equivalents**
- De Winter T-waves (upsloping ST depression with tall T-waves in anterior leads)
- Wellens syndrome (biphasic or deeply inverted T-waves in V2-V3, indicating critical LAD stenosis)
- Posterior MI (ST depression in V1-V3, which may be the only finding when posterior leads are not obtained)
- Right ventricular MI (ST elevation in V4R, must be specifically sought with right-sided leads)
- New or presumably new left bundle branch block (LBBB) with Sgarbossa criteria positive

**Failure to Obtain Serial ECGs**
- A single normal ECG does not rule out ACS. The standard of care requires repeat ECGs when symptoms persist or recur, and when troponin trends are concerning.

**Failure to Compare with Prior ECGs**
- New ST-T wave changes that would be apparent on comparison may be missed when prior ECGs are not reviewed.

## Risk Stratification: The HEART Score and Beyond

The HEART score has become the dominant risk stratification tool in ED chest pain evaluation. It assesses five components, each scored 0-2:

- **H**istory (highly suspicious, moderately suspicious, slightly suspicious)
- **E**CG (significant ST deviation, nonspecific changes, normal)
- **A**ge (>65, 45-65, <45)
- **R**isk factors (>3, 1-2, none)
- **T**roponin (>3x normal, 1-3x normal, normal)

**Score Interpretation and Standard of Care Implications**

- Score 0-3 (low risk): candidates for early discharge with outpatient follow-up, provided serial troponins are negative
- Score 4-6 (intermediate risk): admission for observation, serial troponins, and further cardiac workup (stress test or coronary CTA)
- Score 7-10 (high risk): admission with cardiology consultation and likely invasive evaluation

**Common Deviations in Risk Stratification**

- Failure to calculate or document a risk score at all
- Underscoring risk factors (missing family history, not recognizing cocaine use as equivalent to CAD risk)
- Discharging intermediate-risk patients (HEART 4-6) without further workup
- Substituting clinical gestalt for structured risk assessment, which has been shown to produce inferior outcomes

## Atypical Presentations: The Highest-Risk Patients

Plaintiff cases often involve patients with atypical ACS presentations who fall outside the classic "crushing chest pain" pattern. These patients are disproportionately affected:

**Women**
- More likely to present with dyspnea, nausea, fatigue, back pain, or jaw pain without classic chest pain
- Studies show women are less likely to receive timely troponin testing and ECGs compared to men presenting with similar risk profiles

**Diabetic Patients**
- Autonomic neuropathy can blunt or eliminate chest pain. "Silent MI" is well-documented in diabetic populations.
- A diabetic patient presenting with dyspnea, diaphoresis, or unexplained fatigue should trigger ACS evaluation.

**Young Adults (<40)**
- The "too young for a heart attack" bias leads to missed diagnoses. Cocaine and methamphetamine use, familial hypercholesterolemia, and hypercoagulable states can cause MI at any age.

**Elderly Patients**
- May present with altered mental status, weakness, syncope, or falls rather than chest pain.

## Evidence Gathering: Building the Record

When evaluating a potential missed ACS case, the following records and evidence are critical:

**Medical Records to Obtain**
- Complete ED record including nursing triage notes with exact times
- All ECGs with timestamps (often stored separately in the ECG management system)
- Laboratory results with order times and result times
- Physician documentation including medical decision-making
- Discharge instructions and follow-up plans
- Hospital ACS/chest pain protocol or order set
- Quality metrics data (if available through discovery)
- Subsequent admission records showing confirmed MI

**Timeline Construction**

Build a minute-by-minute timeline including:
1. Symptom onset time (from patient history)
2. ED arrival and triage time
3. Time to first ECG
4. Time of troponin order vs. time blood drawn vs. time resulted
5. Time of any repeat testing
6. Time of disposition decision
7. Time of actual discharge
8. Time of return visit or subsequent cardiac event

**Deviation Markers to Flag**
- Single troponin drawn with discharge before second draw resulted
- No ECG within 10 minutes of arrival
- HEART score not documented
- Discharge of intermediate or high-risk patient without further workup
- No mention of ACS on the differential diagnosis
- Failure to document reassessment after negative initial workup
- Discharge instructions that do not include specific return precautions for cardiac symptoms

## Defense Arguments to Anticipate

**Hindsight Bias**
- The defense will argue that the diagnosis is only obvious in retrospect. Counter with: the standard of care requires serial testing precisely because the diagnosis is not always obvious at presentation. The guidelines exist to systematically catch what clinical judgment alone may miss.

**Atypical Presentation**
- The defense will argue the patient did not present with classic chest pain. Counter with: atypical presentations are well-documented in the literature and guidelines. A competent emergency physician is expected to maintain ACS on the differential for patients with risk factors, even without classic symptoms.

**Normal Initial ECG and Troponin**
- The defense will point to normal initial results. Counter with: serial testing is required specifically because initial values may be normal early in the course of ACS. A single normal result is insufficient to rule out the diagnosis.

**Patient Left Against Medical Advice (AMA)**
- If applicable, the defense may argue the patient left AMA. Counter with: examine whether the physician adequately explained the risks of leaving, and whether the AMA documentation meets legal requirements.

**Intervening Causes**
- The defense may argue that even with proper evaluation, the outcome would have been the same. Counter with: quantitative evidence on survival and morbidity differences with timely intervention. For STEMI, mortality increases approximately 7.5% for each 30-minute delay in reperfusion.

## Expert Witness Considerations

**Specialty Selection**
- Board-certified emergency medicine physicians are the most appropriate experts for standard-of-care opinions regarding ED chest pain evaluation
- Interventional cardiologists are valuable for opinions on causation (what timely intervention would have achieved)
- Avoid general practitioners or non-ED specialists for standard-of-care testimony -- the defense will challenge their qualification to opine on ED practice

**Key Qualifications**
- Active clinical practice in emergency medicine (not retired)
- Board certification in emergency medicine (ABEM)
- Experience with current troponin protocols and ACS guidelines
- Publication record in relevant areas (helpful but not required)
- No history of disciplinary actions
- Practice in a similar clinical setting (community ED vs. academic center)

## Damages Considerations

Missed ACS cases can produce substantial damages due to the severity and permanence of cardiac injury:

- **Myocardial damage**: quantifiable via ejection fraction reduction, troponin peak levels, and cardiac MRI findings
- **Procedural morbidity**: delayed catheterization, emergency CABG, mechanical circulatory support
- **Chronic heart failure**: lifetime treatment costs, reduced functional capacity, life expectancy reduction
- **Wrongful death**: when delayed diagnosis leads to fatal arrhythmia or cardiogenic shock
- **Lost earning capacity**: cardiac disability preventing return to physically demanding occupations

## Actionable Takeaways for Plaintiff Counsel

1. **Request the hospital's chest pain or ACS protocol** in discovery. Deviation from the institution's own protocol is powerful evidence.
2. **Reconstruct the timeline** with minute-level precision. The gap between troponin order, blood draw, result, and discharge tells the story.
3. **Identify the specific troponin assay** used. High-sensitivity vs. conventional assays have different cutoffs and required intervals.
4. **Calculate the HEART score** from the documented history. If the physician did not document it, calculate it yourself and demonstrate it warranted further evaluation.
5. **Obtain all ECGs** including any performed by EMS in the field. ECG changes can be transient and an abnormal prehospital ECG followed by a normal ED ECG is highly significant.
6. **Review the nursing notes independently** from the physician note. Nurses often document symptoms, vital sign trends, and clinical observations that the physician note omits.
7. **Establish the counterfactual**: retained a cardiologist who can testify to what timely intervention would have achieved, quantifying the harm caused by delay.
