## What Is Sepsis?

Sepsis is your body's extreme and life-threatening response to an infection. Instead of fighting the infection in one place, your immune system goes into overdrive and starts attacking your own organs. Sepsis can develop from any type of infection -- a urinary tract infection, pneumonia, a skin wound, or even a dental abscess.

Sepsis is a medical emergency. When treated quickly with antibiotics and IV fluids (fluids given directly into your veins), most patients survive. But when treatment is delayed, sepsis can rapidly progress to septic shock (dangerously low blood pressure), organ failure, and death. **Every hour that antibiotics are delayed increases the risk of death by approximately 4-8%**.

This is why delayed sepsis treatment in the emergency room is one of the most common -- and most devastating -- forms of medical malpractice.

## How Common Is Sepsis?

Sepsis affects more than 1.7 million adults in the United States each year and causes approximately 350,000 deaths. It is the leading cause of death in hospitals and the most expensive condition treated in American hospitals.

Despite how common and dangerous it is, sepsis is frequently missed or undertreated in the emergency room. Studies show that up to 30% of sepsis patients experience delays in appropriate treatment, and delayed treatment is associated with significantly higher mortality rates.

## What the Standard of Care Requires

When you arrive at an emergency room with signs of possible sepsis, the medical standard of care requires your doctors and nurses to act quickly. The key guidelines come from the Surviving Sepsis Campaign (SSC) and the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) SEP-1 measure:

**Within the First Hour (the "Hour-1 Bundle")**

- **Measure lactate level**: Lactate (a blood test) measures how well your organs are getting oxygen. An elevated lactate level is a warning sign of sepsis.
- **Obtain blood cultures**: Before giving antibiotics, blood samples should be drawn to identify the specific bacteria causing the infection.
- **Start broad-spectrum antibiotics**: Antibiotics that cover a wide range of bacteria should be given within one hour of recognizing sepsis. This is the single most time-critical intervention.
- **Begin IV fluid resuscitation**: If your blood pressure is low or your lactate is elevated, you should receive an initial bolus of 30 mL/kg (roughly 2-3 liters for an average adult) of crystalloid fluid.
- **Start vasopressors if needed**: If your blood pressure remains low after IV fluids, medications to raise your blood pressure (vasopressors) should be started.

**Within the First Three Hours**

- **Reassess fluid status**: Your doctor should evaluate whether you need additional fluids based on your blood pressure, heart rate, urine output, and other measures.
- **Repeat lactate if initially elevated**: A repeat lactate test helps determine whether treatment is working.

These timelines are not suggestions -- they are the standard of care recognized by virtually every emergency medicine organization, hospital, and regulatory body. Failure to meet these benchmarks, especially the one-hour antibiotic target, constitutes a deviation from accepted medical practice.

## Warning Signs That Your Care Fell Below Standard

If you or a loved one was treated in the ER for sepsis (or what turned out to be sepsis), the following situations may indicate that care fell below the accepted standard:

- **Antibiotics were delayed more than one hour** after you arrived or after sepsis was suspected. Check your medical records for the time of arrival, the time sepsis was identified, and the time antibiotics were administered.
- **Sepsis was not recognized despite classic signs.** If you had a fever (or abnormally low temperature), rapid heart rate, rapid breathing, confusion, and a known infection, and the ER did not identify sepsis, that is a serious concern.
- **No lactate was drawn.** A lactate blood test is a basic part of sepsis screening. If it was never ordered, your sepsis workup was incomplete.
- **No blood cultures were drawn before antibiotics.** While this omission is less harmful to you directly (antibiotics should not be delayed to wait for cultures), it does suggest the sepsis protocol was not followed properly.
- **You were sent home with sepsis.** Patients with sepsis require hospital admission for IV antibiotics and monitoring. Being discharged from the ER with unrecognized sepsis is a critical failure.
- **IV fluids were not given or were delayed.** If your blood pressure was low and you did not receive aggressive fluid resuscitation, that is a deviation from standard care.
- **Vital signs were not monitored frequently.** Sepsis patients should have vital signs checked at least every 1-2 hours, and more frequently if unstable.

## Who Is Most at Risk for Missed Sepsis?

Certain patients are more likely to have their sepsis missed or undertreated:

- **Elderly patients**: Older adults may not develop a fever with sepsis and may present primarily with confusion or weakness, leading doctors to attribute their symptoms to other causes.
- **Patients with weakened immune systems**: Including those on chemotherapy, organ transplant recipients, and people with HIV/AIDS.
- **Infants and young children**: Pediatric sepsis can present subtly with poor feeding, irritability, or unusual sleepiness. It requires a high index of suspicion.
- **Patients with urinary tract infections**: UTIs are one of the most common sources of sepsis, particularly in elderly women, and the severity can escalate rapidly.
- **Patients who "look well" initially**: Some patients with early sepsis may appear relatively well at triage, leading to lower priority and longer waits before evaluation.

## What Happens When Sepsis Treatment Is Delayed

The consequences of delayed sepsis treatment can be catastrophic:

- **Organ failure**: Sepsis can cause your kidneys, liver, lungs, and heart to shut down. Some patients require dialysis or mechanical ventilation.
- **Amputation**: In severe cases (particularly with septic shock), reduced blood flow to the extremities can lead to tissue death, requiring amputation of fingers, toes, hands, or feet.
- **Prolonged ICU stay**: Patients with severe sepsis often spend weeks in the intensive care unit, frequently requiring multiple invasive procedures.
- **Long-term disability**: Survivors of severe sepsis often experience lasting fatigue, pain, cognitive difficulties (sometimes called "brain fog"), and post-traumatic stress.
- **Death**: Sepsis and septic shock have mortality rates ranging from 15% to over 40%, depending on severity. Delayed treatment significantly increases these numbers.

## What to Do If You Suspect a Problem

If you or a loved one experienced a bad outcome after being treated for sepsis in the ER, or if you believe sepsis was missed entirely, take these steps:

1. **Request your complete medical records.** This includes the ER record, nursing notes, lab results with timestamps, medication administration records, and vital sign flowsheets. The timestamps are critical -- they tell the story of how quickly (or slowly) care was provided.

2. **Look at the timeline.** Identify:
   - When you arrived at the ER
   - When your vital signs were first taken
   - When a lactate level was ordered and resulted
   - When blood cultures were drawn
   - When antibiotics were first given
   - When IV fluids were started

3. **Note the gaps.** If more than one hour passed between the time sepsis should have been recognized and the time antibiotics were given, that gap is medically significant.

4. **Preserve everything.** Keep all medical records, discharge papers, pharmacy records, and any communications with the hospital. If your loved one passed away, request the autopsy report and death certificate.

5. **Write down your recollection.** As soon as possible, write down what you remember about the ER visit -- what symptoms you reported, what the doctors and nurses told you, how long you waited, and any concerns you raised that were dismissed.

## When to Consult an Attorney

You should consult a medical malpractice attorney if:

- A loved one died from sepsis after an ER visit where treatment was delayed
- You or a family member suffered organ failure, amputation, or prolonged ICU stay following a delayed sepsis diagnosis
- You were sent home from the ER with what turned out to be sepsis and later required emergency readmission
- You believe the ER failed to recognize obvious signs of sepsis (high fever, rapid heart rate, confusion, known infection)
- Antibiotics were significantly delayed after ER arrival

Sepsis malpractice cases are supported by clear, time-based guidelines that make deviations relatively straightforward to prove. The CMS SEP-1 measure and the Surviving Sepsis Campaign guidelines provide specific, measurable benchmarks that your attorney and expert witnesses can use to evaluate whether your care met the standard.

Medical malpractice attorneys who handle sepsis cases typically offer free initial consultations and work on contingency. The statute of limitations (the deadline for filing a claim) varies by state, so it is important to consult an attorney sooner rather than later.

## The Bottom Line

Sepsis is a time-critical emergency where every hour matters. The standard of care requires rapid recognition, immediate antibiotics, IV fluids, and close monitoring. When these steps are delayed or missed, the consequences can be devastating. If you or someone you love suffered a serious outcome from sepsis and you have questions about whether the care was adequate, you have every right to seek answers.
