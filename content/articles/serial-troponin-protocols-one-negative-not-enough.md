## Clinical Foundation: Why Serial Testing Is Required

The cardiac troponin assay is the biochemical cornerstone of acute myocardial infarction (AMI) diagnosis. However, troponin kinetics mandate serial measurement. The protein is released from injured cardiomyocytes in a time-dependent fashion -- initial serum levels may be undetectable for 1-3 hours after the onset of myocardial necrosis, even with high-sensitivity assays. This biological reality underpins the universal guideline requirement that no patient be ruled out for ACS based on a single troponin value.

For plaintiff counsel, the single-troponin discharge is one of the cleanest and most provable deviations in emergency medicine malpractice. The guidelines are unambiguous, the deviation is documented in the medical record itself (only one troponin drawn), and the causal chain from missed MI to adverse cardiac outcome is well-established.

## Current Troponin Assays and Their Protocols

Understanding which assay the defendant hospital uses is essential because each assay type dictates a specific serial testing protocol.

**High-Sensitivity Troponin I (hs-cTnI)**

The Abbott ARCHITECT and Siemens Atellica assays are the most widely deployed in U.S. hospitals.

- **0/1-hour protocol (ESC 2020)**: Draw at presentation (T0) and at 1 hour (T1). Rule-out requires both values below assay-specific thresholds AND delta below threshold. Patients in the "observe zone" require a third draw at 3 hours.
- **0/2-hour protocol**: Alternative rapid protocol validated for some assays. Same principle: two values required minimum.
- **0/3-hour protocol (AHA/ACC 2021)**: Draw at T0 and T3. Both values must be below the 99th percentile upper reference limit (URL) with no significant delta.

**High-Sensitivity Troponin T (hs-cTnT)**

The Roche Elecsys assay, widely used internationally and increasingly in the U.S.

- Rule-out threshold at T0: <5 ng/L with symptom onset >3 hours ago (single draw may be sufficient ONLY in this narrow circumstance)
- If symptom onset <3 hours: mandatory serial testing at 0 and 1 or 3 hours
- Delta >3-5 ng/L between draws is considered significant rise/fall

**Conventional Troponin (non-high-sensitivity)**

Still in use at some hospitals, particularly smaller or rural facilities.

- **0/6-hour protocol**: Minimum of two draws separated by 6 hours
- **0/3-6-hour protocol**: Some institutions use a 3-hour interval with a confirmatory 6-hour draw for borderline cases
- Single negative conventional troponin has a miss rate of up to 15-20% for NSTEMI

**Key Litigation Implication**: Request the hospital's laboratory reference ranges and assay specifications in discovery. The specific assay determines which protocol applies and what intervals are required. A deviation from the assay-appropriate protocol is a deviation from the standard of care.

## The Evidence Base Against Single-Troponin Discharge

The prohibition against single-troponin discharge is supported by multiple layers of evidence:

**Guideline Statements**

- **AHA/ACC 2021 Chest Pain Guideline**: "Serial cardiac troponin measurements should be obtained to identify a rising and/or falling pattern." (Class I, Level of Evidence A)
- **ESC 2020 NSTE-ACS Guideline**: Serial testing is embedded in every recommended algorithm. No rule-out pathway permits a single value without qualifying conditions.
- **ACEP Clinical Policy 2018 (reaffirmed 2024)**: Level B recommendation for serial troponin testing. Notes that "a single troponin... is insufficient to exclude ACS."

**Clinical Studies**

- Shah et al., *Lancet* 2015: Demonstrated that implementation of hs-cTn serial protocols reduced missed MI rates from 3% to <1%.
- Body et al., *BMJ* 2011: The MACS study showed that clinical assessment combined with serial troponin outperformed any single-point-in-time strategy.
- Than et al., *Annals of Emergency Medicine* 2012: The ADAPT study established that the combination of TIMI score, ECG, and serial troponin could safely identify low-risk patients -- but only with serial values.

**CMS and Regulatory Standards**

While CMS does not have a specific quality measure for serial troponin testing (unlike the SEP-1 sepsis measure), the Joint Commission and many state regulators include chest pain protocol compliance in quality assessments. Hospital accreditation standards increasingly reference AHA/ACC guidelines as the expected standard.

## Common Deviation Patterns

In plaintiff cases involving single-troponin discharge, the following patterns emerge repeatedly:

**Pattern 1: Premature Discharge**

The physician orders a troponin at presentation, sees a negative result within 1-2 hours, and discharges the patient before the recommended interval for a repeat draw. The medical record typically shows:
- ED length of stay under 3 hours for a chest pain patient
- Single troponin drawn and resulted
- No documentation of a plan for serial testing
- Discharge instructions that may or may not include return precautions

**Pattern 2: Protocol Override Without Documentation**

The hospital has a chest pain order set that includes serial troponin testing, but the physician cancels the second draw or discharges the patient before it is due. Key discovery requests:
- The hospital's chest pain or ACS order set
- Any modification or cancellation of pending orders
- Nursing notes documenting orders for serial labs

**Pattern 3: Risk Score Misapplication**

The physician documents a low HEART score or TIMI score and uses it to justify early discharge without serial troponin. However, careful review often reveals:
- Underscoring of risk factors (missed family history, undocumented smoking status)
- Application of the risk score before serial troponin results were available (the "T" in HEART score requires the troponin trend, not a single value)
- HEART score of 4-6 (intermediate risk) inappropriately managed as low-risk

**Pattern 4: Atypical Presentation Bias**

The physician attributes the patient's symptoms to a non-cardiac cause (musculoskeletal pain, anxiety, GERD) and either does not order troponin at all or orders a single value to "rule out." This is particularly common in:
- Women presenting with dyspnea, nausea, or fatigue without classic chest pain
- Young adults where "too young for MI" bias prevails
- Patients with a known psychiatric history whose somatic complaints are dismissed

**Pattern 5: System Failure**

The physician orders serial troponin, but a system failure prevents the second draw:
- The patient is moved from the ED to a hallway or observation area and the order is lost in handoff
- Nursing staffing shortages delay the blood draw, and the physician discharges before it is completed
- The result is available in the EMR but the physician does not review it before finalizing the discharge

## Evidence Gathering Strategy

**Essential Discovery Requests**

1. **Hospital Chest Pain / ACS Protocol**: The written protocol that governs the evaluation of chest pain patients in the defendant's ED. This is the most powerful single document in many cases.

2. **Order Set Configuration**: The EMR order set for chest pain evaluation, including default orders and whether serial troponin is a default or optional component.

3. **Laboratory Records**: Complete troponin ordering history including:
   - Time troponin was ordered
   - Time blood was drawn (phlebotomy timestamp)
   - Time result was available in the EMR
   - Whether additional troponin orders were placed and subsequently cancelled

4. **Audit Trail / EMR Access Logs**: When did the physician open and close the patient's chart? Did they access the troponin result before signing the discharge? Many EMR systems (Epic, Cerner) maintain detailed access logs that can be subpoenaed.

5. **Nursing Documentation**: Nurses frequently document when orders are due, when blood is drawn, and when patients are discharged. These timestamps are often more granular than physician documentation.

6. **Discharge Summary and Instructions**: Review for any mention of return precautions specific to cardiac symptoms and any instructions to follow up for additional troponin testing (an unusual but occasionally seen practice that effectively acknowledges the workup was incomplete).

7. **Quality and Peer Review Records**: While often protected by state peer review privilege, attempt to obtain any quality metrics related to chest pain door-to-discharge times, serial troponin compliance rates, or bounceback analysis (patients who return within 72 hours with ACS).

## Defense Arguments and Counter-Strategies

**Defense: "The troponin was negative."**

Counter: A single negative troponin does not rule out ACS. This is explicitly stated in every major guideline. The troponin was negative at that moment in time because the assay had not yet detected the evolving myocardial injury. Serial testing exists precisely because early troponins can be falsely reassuring.

**Defense: "The patient had a low risk score."**

Counter: The HEART score and TIMI score require serial troponin values for accurate calculation. A HEART score calculated with a single troponin is incomplete. Furthermore, even low-risk patients (HEART 0-3) have a 0.9-1.7% 30-day MACE rate, which is why serial troponin testing remains part of the protocol.

**Defense: "The patient's symptoms resolved."**

Counter: Symptom resolution does not rule out ACS. Unstable angina by definition involves transient episodes of ischemia with symptom-free intervals. The standard of care requires objective biochemical confirmation (serial troponin) regardless of symptom trajectory.

**Defense: "The patient had a non-cardiac presentation."**

Counter: Atypical presentations of ACS are well-documented in the literature, particularly in women, diabetics, and the elderly. A competent emergency physician is expected to maintain ACS on the differential and perform objective testing, not rely solely on symptom characteristics.

**Defense: "Our hospital protocol permits single-troponin discharge for very low-risk patients."**

Counter: Review the protocol carefully. Most protocols that reference single-troponin pathways require very specific qualifying criteria (hs-cTnT <5 ng/L with symptom onset >3 hours, low HEART score, normal ECG, age <50). If the patient did not meet every criterion, the protocol was not followed. Additionally, the protocol itself may not meet the national standard of care if it deviates from AHA/ACC or ESC guidelines.

## Expert Witness Selection

**For Standard of Care Testimony**

- Board-certified emergency medicine physician (ABEM)
- Active clinical practice in an ED that uses the same or similar troponin assay
- Familiarity with current AHA/ACC and ESC guidelines on chest pain evaluation
- Experience with HEART score and serial troponin protocols
- Ideally: involvement in developing or teaching chest pain protocols at their institution

**For Causation Testimony**

- Interventional cardiologist or cardiac intensivist
- Can testify to what timely diagnosis and intervention would have achieved
- Can quantify myocardial damage (ejection fraction reduction, troponin peak as marker of infarct size)
- Can provide mortality and morbidity statistics for timely vs. delayed reperfusion

**Expert Coordination**

The most effective case presentation pairs an emergency medicine expert (standard of care) with a cardiology expert (causation and damages). The EM expert establishes what should have been done; the cardiology expert establishes what the outcome would have been with appropriate care.

## Damages Framework

**Quantifiable Cardiac Injury**

- Peak troponin level (correlates with infarct size)
- Ejection fraction reduction (baseline vs. post-MI)
- Cardiac MRI findings (infarct territory, microvascular obstruction)
- Need for revascularization (PCI or CABG)
- Need for device implantation (ICD, CRT)
- Development of heart failure symptoms (NYHA classification)

**Mortality Data for Counterfactual Analysis**

- STEMI: each 30-minute delay in door-to-balloon time increases mortality by approximately 7.5%
- NSTEMI: delayed intervention is associated with 2-3x increased 6-month mortality in high-risk patients
- For patients who survive the initial event: 5-year mortality for heart failure with reduced EF remains approximately 50%

## Actionable Takeaways

1. **The single-troponin discharge is indefensible under current guidelines.** If the record shows one troponin drawn with discharge before a second draw, you have a clear breach.

2. **Identify the specific assay** through discovery. Each assay has a defined protocol with specific intervals. Match the protocol to the record.

3. **Obtain the hospital's own protocol.** Deviation from the institution's protocol is often more powerful than deviation from national guidelines, because it eliminates the defense argument that "different institutions do it differently."

4. **Build the timeline with lab system timestamps**, not physician note times. Lab system timestamps (order placed, specimen received, result released) are objective and immutable.

5. **Pair EM and cardiology experts** for maximum impact: the EM expert for breach, the cardiologist for causation and damages quantification.

6. **Calculate the HEART score yourself** from the medical record. If the physician did not document one, calculate it and show it required further evaluation. If they did document one, verify the scoring is accurate.

7. **Check for prior ECGs** in the hospital system. New ST-T wave changes visible only on comparison are frequently missed when prior ECGs are not reviewed.
